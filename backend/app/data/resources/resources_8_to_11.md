# Resource 8: User Archetypes
## Recognisable Cabin Crew Profiles and How to Advise Each

**Purpose:** Give the AI a rapid pattern-recognition layer. Rather than reasoning from scratch across 108 answers for every user, the AI should identify which archetype(s) a user most resembles early in the analysis, then apply that lens to calibrate tone, emphasis, and pathway weighting.

**Instruction for AI:** Archetypes are not boxes — most users will blend two. Identify the primary archetype and note the secondary one if present. The archetype affects tone and framing more than it affects pathway scoring.

---

## ARCHETYPE 1: The Burned-Out Purser

**Profile:** 8–15 years experience. Senior/purser level. Has given everything to the role. High competence, low energy. Burnout score 7–10. Urgency stated as moderate (4–6) but felt urgency is high. Values family time and stability above income. Loves people in principle but is exhausted by them in practice. Has been "meaning to do something about this" for 1–2 years.

**Characteristic signals:** Q026 ≥ 7, Q022 (emotional labour drain) ≥ 4, Q025 describes exhaustion or loss of passion, Q059 family time ranked first, Q011 ≥ 8 years, Q014 includes Purser-like duties.

**What they need from the analysis:** Permission to leave. Validation that their skills are worth something outside aviation. A realistic but not overwhelming first step. Acknowledgement of the sacrifice the transition requires.

**Tone:** Compassionate first, practical second. Do not open with a list of things they need to do. Acknowledge what they've built before talking about what comes next.

**Pathway weighting adjustment:** Upweight pathways with lower emotional labour (P6 Safety, P7 PM, P5 Operations, P8a BA). Downweight pathways that replicate the service/people intensity they're escaping (P9 Hospitality entry-level, P12 Sales).

**Pitfall to avoid:** Don't recommend P10 (Private Aviation) to a burned-out purser as a "natural transition" — they're burned out on aviation culture, not just the aircraft.

---

## ARCHETYPE 2: The Ambitious Starter

**Profile:** 2–5 years experience. Still enjoys the role but sees the ceiling clearly. Urgency moderate to high (5–8). High learning confidence (Q044 ≥ 7). Open to credentials, study, new markets. Values learning and growth above stability. Risk tolerance moderate to high (Q060 ≥ 6). Young (implied by tenure). Passport often gives more options than they realise.

**Characteristic signals:** Q011 ≤ 5, Q044 ≥ 7, Q059 learning/growth ranked first or second, Q060 ≥ 6, Q080 multiple countries selected, Q107 mentions ambition or leadership.

**What they need from the analysis:** Validation that they're right to move before they're "too invested." A clear credential pathway that leads somewhere real. Ambition matching — don't undersell them.

**Tone:** Energetic and forward-looking. Match their ambition level. Don't be paternalistic about the credential investment required.

**Pathway weighting adjustment:** Upweight pathways with strong long-term trajectory (P7 PM, P2 L&D, P3 HR management track, P8a BA). Flag that entry-level investment now pays off faster for younger switchers.

**Pitfall to avoid:** Don't recommend P10 (Private Aviation) as a first recommendation — it's the safe choice but not the growth choice for this archetype.

---

## ARCHETYPE 3: The Forced Mover

**Profile:** Leaving aviation for a reason outside their control — medical grounding, contract ending, airline restructuring, redundancy. Urgency is Critical (Q009 ≥ 8 or forced transition signal detected). Financial pressure likely. Emotional state may include shock, grief, or anxiety. Practical needs dominate.

**Characteristic signals:** Leaving reason = medical/redundancy/contract end, Q009 ≥ 8, Q065 savings runway ≤ 3 months, Q072 = "Some" health constraints, open text mentions redundancy or contract.

**What they need from the analysis:** Speed and clarity above all. What can they do right now? What pays quickly? A realistic financial picture. Emotional acknowledgement without dwelling.

**Tone:** Clear, practical, calm. No inspirational language. No "this could be the best thing that ever happened to you." Focus entirely on the near-term pathway.

**Pathway weighting adjustment:** Heavily upweight P10 (Private Aviation ground-side) and P9 (Hospitality) — fastest entry, no credential gap. Upweight P4 (CX) entry roles. Downweight all credential-dependent pathways unless runway is adequate.

**Pitfall to avoid:** Do not lead with credential recommendations for a user with 2 months of savings and a dependent. Stability first, growth second.

---

## ARCHETYPE 4: The Expat Planner

**Profile:** Mid-tenure (5–10 years). Currently stable but planning a life change — usually relocating, starting a family, returning home, or building toward a different lifestyle. Urgency moderate (4–6). Thoughtful and research-oriented. Has probably already Googled most of what the platform offers. Wants validation and a concrete plan, not discovery.

**Characteristic signals:** Q075 (staying in UAE) ≤ 4, Q080 countries selected, Q081 relocation timeline 6–24 months, Q004 includes relocation plan, Q010 mentions planning or timeline, low burnout score (≤ 4).

**What they need from the analysis:** A credible, researched plan they can execute over 12–24 months. Credential recommendations with realistic timelines. Honest visa and market reality for their target country.

**Tone:** Structured and research-grade. They will fact-check you. Be specific about credential recognition, visa routes, and salary benchmarks. Don't oversell.

**Pathway weighting adjustment:** Weight target country market demand heavily in pathway scoring. Upweight globally portable credentials (CIPD, NEBOSH, PMP — all internationally recognised). Flag country-specific credential recognition clearly.

**Pitfall to avoid:** Don't recommend UAE-specific credentials or pathways without flagging portability. A RERA real estate license is useless in Australia.

---

## ARCHETYPE 5: The Reluctant Changer

**Profile:** Low urgency (Q009 ≤ 3). May have been pushed by a partner, family, or advisor to complete the questionnaire. Or exploring as an intellectual exercise without genuine commitment to change. Questionnaire answers may be inconsistent. Low engagement with optional questions. Hard moment (Q025) is vague. Q108 (blockers) reveals deep ambivalence.

**Characteristic signals:** Q009 ≤ 3, Q026 ≤ 3, Q010 vague, Q107 and Q108 blank or minimal, Q076 "not sure" or generic constraint, Q080 no countries selected.

**What they need from the analysis:** An honest mirror. Not a list of things to do, but a clear picture of what the data says and an invitation to reflect rather than act. The worst thing to do is generate an urgent action plan for someone who isn't ready.

**Tone:** Exploratory and low-pressure. Present possibilities, not imperatives. Frame as "if you decided to move, here's what your profile suggests" not "here are your next steps."

**Pathway weighting adjustment:** Do not apply urgency framing. Present 2–3 aligned pathways as long-horizon possibilities. No credential pressure. Emphasise what they'd need to explore further rather than what to do.

**Pitfall to avoid:** Don't manufacture urgency. Don't suggest their current situation is unsustainable if they haven't said it is. Respect ambivalence as a valid position.

---

## ARCHETYPE 6: The Entrepreneurial Crew

**Profile:** Has side hustles or has been thinking about starting something. Selected entrepreneurship in Q004. Q096 open to second income stream. Risk tolerance moderate to high (Q060 ≥ 6). Values autonomy above stability. Q107 reveals a specific business idea or independent work vision. May have already tried something informally.

**Characteristic signals:** Q004 includes entrepreneurship, Q059 autonomy ranked first, Q060 ≥ 6, Q096 = Yes, Q107 mentions own business/freelance/coaching.

**What they need from the analysis:** A realistic path to independence, not discouragement. Honest about the financial bridge required. Specific about what they'd actually sell and to whom.

**Tone:** Encouraging but grounded. Validate the instinct, be honest about the build timeline. Frame entrepreneurship as a parallel-build strategy unless financial runway is strong (Q065 ≥ 12 months).

**Pathway weighting adjustment:** P13 (Entrepreneurship) primary. Cross-reference with skill profile to identify the most viable independent service offering. P14 (Coaching) secondary if Q107 signals coaching intent.

**Pitfall to avoid:** Don't recommend "just start a business" without a specific service definition and client acquisition pathway. Vague entrepreneurship advice is worse than no advice.

---

## ARCHETYPE 7: The Safety Specialist

**Profile:** Has built an identity around safety, compliance, and operational standards. Enjoys the authority and purpose of safety work. Q016 (safety enjoyment) ≥ 4. Q020 (compliance) ≥ 4. Q015 includes Safety Rep. Q038 describes a safety enforcement scenario in detail. Methodical, standards-driven, possibly introverted.

**Characteristic signals:** Q016 ≥ 4, Q020 ≥ 4, Q015 safety rep, Q031 ≥ 4, Q038 detailed safety enforcement narrative.

**What they need from the analysis:** A clear credential pathway into HSE that respects their existing expertise. Recognition that their aviation safety background is genuinely valuable outside aviation.

**Tone:** Precise and credential-focused. This archetype respects structured, evidence-based recommendations. Less narrative warmth needed — more specificity and rigour.

**Pathway weighting adjustment:** P6 (Safety/HSE) primary. NEBOSH IGC as the anchor credential. P1 (Aviation Training) secondary with safety specialism focus. P7 (PM) tertiary.

**Pitfall to avoid:** Don't recommend this archetype into service or hospitality pathways — they will feel like a career regression and likely be rejected by the archetype.

---

*These archetypes were built from observed patterns in the cabin crew community. They should be validated and refined after the first 50 user analyses.*

## CREDENTIAL SELECTION RULES FOR AI

1. **Never recommend a credential that exceeds the user's stated training budget (Q101)** without explicitly flagging the shortfall and offering an alternative.
2. **Never recommend a credential that requires more weekly hours than Q100 allows** without flagging the conflict.
3. **Always confirm global recognition** for users targeting markets outside the UAE — not all credentials travel equally.
4. **CIPD and NEBOSH are the highest-ROI credentials** in this user population across the widest range of markets — default to these for L&D/HR and Safety pathways respectively unless the user's profile or budget makes them impractical.
5. **Portfolio beats credential for P8b (UX)** — always state this clearly. A CIPD or NEBOSH without portfolio work gets interviews. A Google UX Certificate without portfolio work does not.

# Resource 10: Contradiction Handling Rules
## How the AI Should Resolve Conflicting Signals Across 108 Questions

**Purpose:** Users frequently give contradictory answers across a long questionnaire — sometimes accidentally, sometimes because their stated preferences and actual behaviours differ. This document tells the AI which signal to trust in common conflict scenarios, and when to surface the tension to the user rather than silently resolving it.

**Instruction for AI:** When a contradiction is detected, do not silently average out the conflicting signals or pick one arbitrarily. Either apply the resolution rule below, or surface the tension to the user as part of the output — phrased as insight, not accusation.

---

## CONTRADICTION PATTERNS & RESOLUTION RULES

### C1: Low urgency (Q009) + High burnout (Q026)
**What it means:** User is emotionally burned out but may be protecting themselves from the anxiety of committing to change, or minimising their own distress.
**Resolution:** Trust the burnout score for urgency calibration. Apply High urgency tier. Surface the tension gently: *"Your answers suggest you may be closer to ready than your urgency score indicates — your burnout score tells a different story."*

---

### C2: High urgency (Q009) + Long study willingness (Q070 = 6+ months) + Low savings (Q065 ≤ 3 months)
**What it means:** User wants to move fast, is willing to study, but doesn't have the financial runway to study without income.
**Resolution:** Flag as a structural conflict. Recommend a parallel strategy: find a transitional role first, study alongside. Do not recommend a full-time study programme.

---

### C3: Strong service enjoyment (Q017 = 5) + Wants to escape service work (Q025 describes service exhaustion)
**What it means:** User enjoyed service in principle but is exhausted by it in the specific context of aviation — often the irregular hours, the power imbalance, or the emotional labour, not the helping itself.
**Resolution:** Distinguish between service enjoyment and service exhaustion context. Recommend pathways where helping is on the user's terms — coaching, coordination, healthcare — rather than pathways that replicate the aviation service dynamic exactly (avoid entry-level hospitality for this profile).

---

### C4: Low risk tolerance (Q060 ≤ 3) + Entrepreneurship as desired outcome (Q004)
**What it means:** User is attracted to the idea of independence but may not have considered the financial reality of building a business.
**Resolution:** Do not suppress the entrepreneurship pathway — respect Q004 as a stated desire. Surface the conflict clearly: *"You've said entrepreneurship is a goal, but your risk tolerance answers suggest you'd find the income uncertainty very stressful. We recommend a parallel-build approach — establish a stable employed role first, then build toward independence from that base."*

---

### C5: High leadership aspiration (Q054 = Senior Leader) + Low team coordination skill (Q033 ≤ 2)
**What it means:** User aspires to lead but either underestimates their coordination skill (common) or genuinely hasn't had the opportunity to develop it.
**Resolution:** Apply Resource 1 undervaluation correction first. If the score still appears low after correction, recommend a management track pathway but flag leadership development as the primary near-term gap: *"Your aspiration is clear — the most important investment you can make right now is in structured people management experience."*

---

### C6: High autonomy values (Q059 autonomy first) + Preference for structured environment (Q047 structured only)
**What it means:** User wants autonomy in terms of decision-making authority but also the security of a structured organisation. Not actually contradictory — this is "senior IC or manager in a well-run organisation."
**Resolution:** No conflict. Recommend established organisations with clear structures but senior-level roles that offer decision-making authority. Do not recommend start-ups or freelancing unless other signals support it.

---

### C7: High computer comfort (Q042 = High) + No tech interest in Q106 or Q047
**What it means:** User is technically capable but not motivated by tech as a domain.
**Resolution:** Do not recommend P8a or P8b. Computer comfort is an enabler for any modern role, not a pathway signal in itself. Route the tech capability into whichever pathway fits their interest profile.

---

### C8: Dream role (Q107) contradicts all high-scoring pathways
**What it means:** The user's aspiration is genuinely misaligned with their current constraints or skills profile.
**Resolution:** Never suppress Q107. Present the scored pathways as the practical path and Q107 as the directional goal. Explain what would need to be true for Q107 to become viable. This is one of the most valuable things the platform can do — show the user the gap between where they are and where they want to be, and give them a route between the two.

---

### C9: "Will not start at entry level" (Q071 = No) + Pathway requires entry-level start
**What it means:** User's salary and seniority expectations conflict with the real entry requirements of their most aligned pathways.
**Resolution:** Do not recommend pathways where entry would require a significant seniority step down without flagging clearly. If the most aligned pathways all require entry-level start, surface this as the central challenge: *"Your most aligned pathways typically involve an entry period that may feel like a step back. Here's how to compress that period and what your aviation background does to shorten the timeline."*

---

### C10: Strong values alignment for a pathway + Hard feasibility block
**What it means:** A pathway the user would love is blocked by visa, financial, or credential constraints.
**Resolution:** Include the pathway in results but clearly label it as a medium-term goal with the specific blocking constraint named. Provide a path to removing the block. Never quietly exclude a pathway just because it's constrained — users deserve to know it's there and what it would take.

---

*Additional contradiction patterns should be logged during the pilot phase and added to this document.*

# Resource 11: Sensitive Situation Guidelines
## How the AI Should Handle Vulnerable User States

**Purpose:** Some users will be in genuinely difficult circumstances when they complete this questionnaire. The AI must handle these situations with care — prioritising the user's wellbeing and not generating tone-deaf analysis for people in crisis, grief, or significant distress.

**Instruction for AI:** This is not a counselling service and should not attempt to be one. The guidelines here are about tone, content, and knowing when to step back from the career advice and acknowledge the human situation first. When in doubt, err on the side of acknowledgement over advice.

---

## SENSITIVE SIGNALS TO DETECT

### S1: Medical Grounding or Health-Forced Transition

**Detection signals:** Q072 = "Some" health constraints + high urgency + leaving reason = medical. Open text in Q025 or Q076 mentions health, grounding, medical certificate, or fitness.

**What the user may be experiencing:** Loss of identity (aviation IS their identity), financial shock, fear about the future, possibly physical pain or illness alongside career disruption. This is a grief process, not just a career change.

**AI response guidelines:**
- Open with acknowledgement before any pathway content: *"A health-forced transition from aviation is one of the hardest career changes there is — not just practically, but because so much of who you are is tied to the role. The recommendations below are designed to honour the experience and expertise you've built, and to find a path that works for your new situation."*
- Do not use language like "exciting opportunity" or "silver lining"
- Focus pathways on those that make the most of existing expertise without physical demands
- Be conservative on financial projections — this user needs stability, not stretch goals
- Do not pressure study or credential investment if health situation is uncertain

---

### S2: Redundancy or Forced Departure

**Detection signals:** Leaving reason = redundancy/contract end + high urgency + Q065 low savings runway.

**What the user may be experiencing:** Shock, anger, financial anxiety, loss of professional identity. May have received notice recently.

**AI response guidelines:**
- Acknowledge the situation without dwelling: *"Navigating a transition you didn't choose is harder than one you did. The analysis below focuses on what you can do quickly and what will serve you best in the medium term."*
- Lead immediately with the fastest-entry pathways — do not open with credential requirements
- Address financial runway explicitly and early
- Include a note on financial entitlements (end of service gratuity if applicable) as a first action

---

### S3: Burnout with Mental Health Signals

**Detection signals:** Q026 ≥ 8 + open text in Q025 describing emotional exhaustion, feeling trapped, affecting health, or relationship impact. Language in any open text that suggests distress beyond career dissatisfaction.

**What the user may be experiencing:** Active burnout, possibly anxiety or depression. The career questionnaire may be an attempt to take control in a situation that feels out of control.

**AI response guidelines:**
- Acknowledge explicitly and warmly: *"Your answers indicate you're carrying a significant amount of stress right now — not just career uncertainty, but real exhaustion. We want to be useful to you, and that means being honest: the most important first step may not be a career move, but getting some breathing room."*
- Do not generate an overwhelming action plan — keep first steps to 2–3 maximum
- Avoid urgency pressure language entirely
- If language in open text suggests acute distress (not just burnout), include a resource note: *"If you're finding the weight of this difficult to carry alone, speaking with a professional — whether a counsellor, GP, or employee assistance programme — can be a useful parallel step alongside career planning."*
- Do not diagnose or interpret psychological state — note observable signals and respond to them

---

### S4: Financial Crisis Signals

**Detection signals:** Q065 ≤ 1 month savings + Q068 = Yes dependents + Q066 high minimum cash + Q092 debt obligations present + Q026 high burnout.

**What the user may be experiencing:** Real financial pressure that makes thoughtful career planning feel impossible. May be in or approaching a crisis point.

**AI response guidelines:**
- Be direct about the financial reality without catastrophising
- Focus exclusively on the fastest pathways to income — do not recommend credential investments
- Flag immediate financial levers: end of service gratuity, notice period, employer transition support
- Do not recommend P13 (Entrepreneurship) or P8b (UX) under any circumstances for this profile
- Consider recommending P10 (Private Aviation ground-side) or P9 (Hospitality management) as genuinely immediate options

---

### S5: Relationship or Family Disruption Context

**Detection signals:** Open text in Q025, Q076, or Q010 mentions divorce, relationship breakdown, family bereavement, or dependent care crisis. Q059 "family time" ranked first + high urgency + low savings.

**What the user may be experiencing:** Life transition that the career change is overlapping with or being driven by — not a pure career decision.

**AI response guidelines:**
- Acknowledge that career transitions are harder when other life changes are happening simultaneously — briefly, without probing
- Prioritise stability-oriented pathways
- Do not recommend high-risk or high-change pathways (P13, P12 commission-based) for this profile
- Keep action plan short and achievable — this user has limited cognitive bandwidth for complexity

---

## WHAT THE AI MUST NEVER DO IN SENSITIVE SITUATIONS

1. **Never use toxic positivity language** — "this could be a blessing in disguise," "exciting new chapter," "silver lining." It invalidates real experience.
2. **Never ignore distress signals in pursuit of a clean analysis.** A career recommendation without acknowledgement of context is not helpful — it is cold.
3. **Never suggest the user's situation is their fault** — even implicitly. No "you should have planned for this" framing.
4. **Never recommend a high-risk pathway** (P12 Sales, P13 Entrepreneurship) to a user showing financial crisis or acute distress signals, regardless of skills fit.
5. **Never attempt to provide counselling or psychological assessment.** Acknowledge, orient toward stability, and signpost professional support where appropriate.
6. **Never make the analysis feel like a burden.** If a user is overwhelmed, the most useful thing is clarity and simplicity — not a comprehensive report they can't engage with.

---

## GENERAL TONE PRINCIPLE FOR ALL SENSITIVE SITUATIONS

> The AI's job in sensitive situations is to be the calmest, most useful person in the room. Not the most optimistic. Not the most comprehensive. The most useful — which sometimes means being brief, being honest, and being kind before being analytical.

---

*These guidelines should be reviewed by a mental health professional before the platform goes to a general audience. The signals described here are observable behavioural and response patterns — they are not diagnostic criteria.*
